# 2. Product Design

For the four hazards, we have determined that each product will provide coverage for one year, until it is repurchased again for the following year. At the beginning of each year, one premium payment will be made, providing GGI with the needed liquidity given the nature of claims under each hazard coverage. Additionally, this structure is favourable as it simplifies the interest assumptions product requirements. Further, in utilising advanced processing technologies, GGI will provide an instantaneous lump sum benefit for every claim, as soon as it is validated by our system.

## 2.1 Business Interruption

BI coverage offers a three-tiered product, segmented by risk, with premiums differing per policy due to the advanced nature of the Q-RISK engine. The three tiers are Core BI Protect, Enhanced BI Plus, Catastrophic BI Ultimate. The product is tiered according to the experience of crews, load volumes and station risk. The premiums and coverage rise with each tier of the product, appearing in Appendix B.1. Tier-specific benefit structure details are provided in Appendix B.1.1.

BI Protect will only cover direct losses from full shutdowns for local stations, includes a 48-hour waiting period, and excludes high risk stations. Enhanced BI Plus adds on cover for partial interruptions, system inefficiencies, and interlinked station issues, includes a 24-hour waiting period, and does not cover multi-system failures. Catastrophic BI Ultimate uniquely covers full system-wide failures, their cascading breakdown effects, and extended periods of shutdown, exhibiting no waiting period. Tier-specific trigger details are provided in Appendix B.1.2.

Applying to all BI coverage tiers, we will not cover losses as a result of gradual deterioration, lack of maintenance, and defects known prior to policy inception. Negligence, operator errors, and failure to meet minimum safety standards/regulations will also be excluded. Coverage does not extend to voluntary shutdowns, planned maintenance shutdowns, or cost-cutting decisions that impact operations. If losses are unable to be verified and records are kept poorly, the coverage will also not be activated. By having strict BI exclusions, we remove high frequency and low severity noise whilst simultaneously preventing moral hazard and the inflation of claims. Given the interconnected nature of stations within the same solar system, traditional interdependence assumptions deteriorate as single BI events can result in multiple claims and huge aggregate losses.

## 2.2 Equipment Failure

We offer CQMC an annual Equipment Failure program with three forms: Equipment Failure Essentials, Equipment Failure Core, and Equipment Failure Plus. Our cover includes repair or replacement costs for sudden accidental breakdown, emergency response costs, and reinstatement expenses. We tier the product by machinery risk. Deductibles, coinsurance, and waiting periods increase with risk, so lower-risk machinery receives lighter terms while higher-risk machinery such as Quantum Bores, Graviton Extractors, and upper-tier Flux Riders / FluxStream Carriers is written on stronger terms and higher annual charged premiums. Detailed machine-level schedules are provided in Appendix C.1.

Covered events include internal mechanical or electrical malfunction, control-system failure, debris or collision impact, radiation-linked malfunction, flare disruption, and thermal shock. We apply this trigger across all three solar systems. We exclude wear and tear, gradual deterioration, deferred maintenance, known defects existing before policy inception, and intentional misuse. Intentional damage and misuse are not covered trigger events. We recognise that the Equipment Failure fleet is not homogeneous. Risk varies materially by machine type and by solar system, so a flat product would underprice upper-tier machinery and overprice lower-tier machinery. We address this by linking product form and underwriting terms directly to modelled risk. As risk rises, we strengthen deductibles, coinsurance, and waiting periods, which makes the product both actuarially priced and operationally disciplined.

## 2.3 Workers’ Compensation

We offer CQMC an annual Workers’ Compensation product with three forms: Workers Compensation ActiveCare Essentials, Workers Compensation ActiveCare, and Workers Compensation ActiveCare Plus. The role-level premiums shown in the Appendix C.1 represent annual premiums allocated per worker segment rather than amounts paid directly by employees. Our premium schedule also reflects more than salary alone: it incorporates role, employment type, predicted claim duration, and operating environment, which makes the pricing structure more defensible than a flat payroll-based schedule. Our cover includes wage replacement, medical treatment, and rehabilitation support. We structure wage replacement using the statutory-style basis adopted in the model, with benefits beginning at a higher replacement rate in the first entitlement period and stepping down in the second entitlement period, subject to the duration cap built into the pricing assumptions (following Australian Labour Laws). Detailed role-level schedules are provided in Appendix D.1.

We trigger Workers’ Compensation cover when a worker suffers a work-related injury or illness during the policy period arising out of or in the course of employment with CQMC. Covered events include machinery-related injury, physical strain injury, transport-related injury, fatigue-related injury, and other occupational injury or illness arising from mining operations. We apply this trigger across all covered worker classes. Wage replacement is paid at 95% of pre-injury weekly earnings for the first 13 weeks of incapacity, then at 80% thereafter while incapacity continues, subject to the statutory weekly maximum (Ð2,662.10) and a benefit cap of 260 weeks for non-severe injuries. Cover also includes medical treatment, rehabilitation support, and permanent disability or death benefits. We exclude fraud, intentional self-harm, and circumstances outside covered employment. We recognise that the main limitation in Workers’ Compensation is not the absence of system-level or worker-segment data, but the amount of extrapolation required. Bayesia System and Oryn Delta require more assumption-setting than Helionis Cluster, and workforce counts are harder to estimate with confidence. We therefore treat workforce counts as modelled pricing exposures rather than official staffing recommendations.

## 2.4 Cargo Loss

For CL, a competitive pricing structure will utilise the variable `Route Risk`, to categorise shipments into 5 levels of riskiness: Low Cargo Cover, Core Transit Cover, Shipment Essentials, High Transit Cover, and Extreme Cargo Cover. CL, unlike most other hazards, does not categorise claims by solar system, thus the scalability of CL Cover to shipments in the new solar systems will be simpler. Another key component of the product is the maximum deductible of Ð31K and a limit of Ð678,000K. See Appendix E.1 for product premiums.

The product provides coverage for external uncontrollable losses during transit. To be covered, the shipment must be accurately documented and contain no fraudulent information. Further, cargo valued below Ð50K or in exceedance Ð680,000K will not be covered, as well as if the container is older than 50 earth years. Importantly, the shipment can only travel for less than 100AU taking at maximum 60 earth months to arrive, otherwise coverage will be redacted.

# 3. Pricing & Capital Modelling

## 3.1 Business Interruption

For BI, the short-term and long-term aggregate losses are right skewed, especially in the long-term scenario, with a long tail of extreme losses to highlight the prevalent tail risk associated with BI. A detailed plot of aggregate loss distributions can be found in Appendix B.2.1, and coinciding important statistics can be found in Appendix A.

In the short-term, BI losses are strongly concentrated around the mean, sitting at roughly Ð652M and it is implied that whilst large losses exist, they exhibit less spread. When observing at the 95th and 99th percentiles, we notice that they are relatively close to the mean, sitting Ð668M and Ð675M respectively, inciting less strain upon short-term capital requirements. In the long-term, the mean aggregate loss skyrocketed to Ð3.5B and there is a wide gap to the 95th and 99th percentiles, valuing Ð3.7B to Ð3.8B respectively, implying substantial accumulation of tail risk. Furthermore the gap between the 95th and 99th percentile is larger than that of the short-term, implying that extreme events become more dangerous over time. This highlights the necessity for robust tail risk management in dealing with BI, especially as the risk profile is dominated by extreme events.

To mimic extreme scenarios, BI space stress was included to capture the effects space specific issues upon business interruptions. These scenarios have the potential to lead to extended downtime, severity spikes, frequency spikes, and correlated losses. Multi-year simulations were run, with spikes added to frequency and severity, but premium values were maintained given their inability to fluctuate with real-time risk. The BI net revenue distribution under space stress can be visualised in Appendix B.2.2, displaying a highly negative net revenue. This 1-in-100 worst case scenario has the propensity to wipe out the entire premium base over and over again in a single hit. This scenario is evidently tail dominated, with the distribution so far skewed that it cannot be covered without huge capital reserves or reinsurance. This is a powerful argument for the implementation of strict hazard-specific exclusions in the BI product designs.

Simulations were run with inflation rates consistently set above 5%, inflating severity, and premiums as a pricing response. When high inflation occurs consistently over a multi-year period, severity increased but the shape of the cost distribution remained similar to that of the baseline, with a slightly thicker yet still manageable tail. Additionally, the tail appears to be smooth, even in the worst-case outcome, net revenue remains positive. This shows the strong resilience of the BI portfolio to parameter shocks.

## 4.2 Business Interruption

In terms of BI, correlated risk scenarios may include system wide shutdowns, forcing many operations to stop simultaneously, supply chain disruptions, and extended recovery periods as replacement parts become limited, due to increased demand, and the interstellar distance slowing transport. Correlation across hazard lines extend into coverage lines, leading to mass increases in claims as the same clients activate multiple policies at once.

Overall, BI scenario testing reveals that when shocks are independent, the product is stable, however it collapses when shocks become correlated.

# 5. Assumptions

A major data-related assumption arose from the inconsistency between the data dictionaries and the actual data, with the value ranges/levels failing to match the given data. It was assumed that the values specified in the data dictionary were more trustworthy and were thus chosen as the basis for data cleaning. So, assumptions were made in order to reverse any systematic input errors. For example, the first crucial assumption made is that all negative numeric values were intended to be positive, where appropriate.

Further, Claim Amount, Cargo Value and Base Salary all had values which significantly exceeded their respective limits. For these variables it was assumed any value above the limit was written in cents rather than dollars and hence was divided by 100. In cases where the variables were below their respective deductibles, they were disregarded. This assumption had a major impact on pricing for the way in which the majority of the raw claim amounts had to be shifted, impacting the scaling of pricing and capital and their projections. Furthermore, for Worker’s Compensation, it was assumed that in the future, the Australian Labour Law regarding work-related injuries and compensation applied galaxy wide.

In cases of missing entries with the data sets, policy IDs were used to map missing values between the frequency and severity sheets where possible. If between the two sheets one had a value outside the defined range while the other was within it - the one in the limits was selected. If both were inside the limits, the conflicting rows would be removed. For other hazard specific assumptions see Appendix B.4 (for BI) and Appendix E.4 (for CL).

# 6. Data and Data Limitations

## Data Limitations

While the data provided is expansive, it contained significant limitations that hindered robust product development. A major data limitation was the aforementioned inconsistency between the value ranges/levels provided in the data dictionaries and that of the actual data. This poses a major threat to our recommendations - if the data dictionaries are incorrect, the severity of every hazard coverage may have been underestimated. Thus the recommended premiums would not be high enough to cover actual costs, rendering the products infeasible.

The historical inflation and interest data is a limitation given its small 15-year period, causing difficulties in understanding true economic patterns. This limits the accuracy of long-term predictions, increasing variability in the models. Additionally, lacking historical environmental data, such as the weather patterns, limit capabilities for predicting claims and hinder understanding of the operating environments. The recommended products assume a stagnated view of the intergalactic mining industry as the models lack the capacity to incorporate effects of technological advancements upon future claim behaviours and additional costs.

Additionally, since the reason for each claim was not provided, the BI coverage triggers and exclusions could not be perfectly tailored to the nature of the claims, thus they have been kept relatively general. This poses a challenge as we are unaware of any common events that could provide great insight into how triggers and exclusions are set to favour net revenue. CL Cover specifically is limited in its success as a product, due to it being unable to provide specific solar system insights. As a result, it could be argued that this product serves more as a guide or temporary solution until we receive access to higher quality data.

Finally, all the aforementioned risks are related to specific operational and environmental issues which might arise in CQMC’s practice. Therefore, it should also be addressed how the insurer will manage its legal risks, especially given CQMC’s history of court issued fines. For example, claims procedures may still differ across countries or even planets and solar systems, so it is currently unclear which law would take precedence. This applies to dispute frameworks and laws which hold us legally liable to a claim. Our limited understanding of how laws operate on an intergalactic scale, impacts our ability to provide recommendations which could be accurately and practically applied.
